# Calisthenics Thesis

Date: 2026-06-08

## Working Thesis

For health, stability, and mobility, calisthenics should function as a whole-body physical capacity practice rather than a hypertrophy or max-strength program.

The first target should be:

- 2 focused sessions per week
- all major muscle groups covered
- a morning floor of up to 15 minutes
- daily or near-daily short mobility, balance, posture, and bone-loading exposure
- clean range of motion
- joint control
- enough strength work to preserve function without compromising cardio, running, or hot yoga

## Definition Of "Optimal"

In this repo, optimal calisthenics means the smallest sustainable dose that meaningfully supports:

- muscular function
- balance
- mobility
- posture
- joint durability
- daily life capacity
- later martial arts practice
- bone health with aging

Optimal does not mean maximum reps, hardest variations, visible muscle gain, or skill chasing.

## Testable Claims

### CAT-001: The weekly floor is 2 focused full-body strength sessions.

Guidelines support muscle-strengthening activities for all major muscle groups on 2 or more days per week.

Status: supported by guidelines.

### CAT-002: Health-first calisthenics should prioritize patterns, not isolated muscles.

The initial pattern set should include squat, posterior-chain control, push, back/posture, trunk, lateral trunk, balance, and mobility.

Status: working hypothesis grounded in guideline intent.

### CAT-003: Progression should start with movement quality, not harder variations.

For this project, progress means smoother reps, better control, better range, better balance, and better recovery before more reps or more difficulty.

Status: working hypothesis.

### CAT-004: Daily movement should be easy unless it is a planned session.

The user wants daily practice, but hard calisthenics every day is not necessary for the health thesis and may compete with recovery.

Status: working hypothesis.

### CAT-005: Mobility and balance should be integrated, not left as optional accessories.

Stability and mobility are primary goals. They need explicit practice and logging.

Status: supported directionally by multicomponent physical activity guidance; exact dose needs research.

### CAT-006: Running and cardio are not enough for bone health by themselves.

Weight-bearing cardio helps, especially brisk walking, running, stairs, and similar loading. But bone-health guidance points toward a combination of weight-bearing exercise, progressive resistance training, posture/back work, and balance training.

Status: supported directionally; personal dose requires testing and bone-status context.

### CAT-007: The morning floor should carry mobility, balance, posture, low-dose bone-loading, push/squat exposure, and core strength.

A daily morning floor of up to 15 minutes is a good place to practice small, repeatable work that supports movement quality without replacing focused sessions. The first version should include cat-cow, a down-dog/plank/push-up flow, a squat pattern, and a rotating core block.

Status: working hypothesis.

### CAT-008: The active personal tier can use higher push-up and squat volume than the starter baseline.

The starter baseline is appropriate for someone new or for deload weeks. The current personal baseline can start higher because the user reports that about 50 regular push-ups and 50 bodyweight squats fit inside a focused session without affecting running.

Status: personal working hypothesis.

Test boundary:

- Accumulate the volume in clean submaximal sets, not max-rep tests.
- Keep 2 to 4 reps in reserve.
- Reduce volume if Wednesday 5K, Thursday hot yoga, sleep, gait, or joint response worsens.
- Do not add targeted modules if the two full-body sessions already feel like enough.

## First Four-Week Test

Weekly targets:

| Week | Focused Sessions | Morning Floor | Progression |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | 2 sessions, 2 rounds | 8-12 minutes mobility, balance, posture | None |
| 2 | 2 sessions, 2 rounds | 10-15 minutes mobility, balance, posture | Improve form notes |
| 3 | 2 sessions, 2-3 rounds if easy | 10-15 minutes plus safe bone-loading exposure | Add reps only if clean |
| 4 | 2 sessions, stable volume | 10-15 minutes, repeatable | Decide after review |

Personal active tier:

- Monday A and Friday B now each have beginner, intermediate, and advanced options.
- Intermediate is the default test because it matches current capacity without forcing advanced variation.
- A2 Intermediate keeps 50 Push-Ups and 50 Bodyweight Squats, then adds Hip Rotations (Push-up Position), CKC Parascapular Exercises, Downward-Facing Dog, calf raises, and lunges.
- B2 Intermediate uses lunges, Push-Ups, Side Lunges, Hip Rotations (Push-up Position), CKC Parascapular Exercises, Modified Side Plank, and calf raises.
- Advanced A/B options are low-volume probes using ACE-linked movements such as Inchworms, High Plank T-Spine Rotation, Push-up with Single-leg Raise, Forward Lunge with Arm Drivers, Squat Jumps, and optional Cycled Split-Squat Jump.
- Optional targeted modules: moving-plank core stability, back/posture, or legs/bone/balance, 6 to 10 minutes, only when recovery is clean.
- Separate equipment layer: 1 weekly pull/load session can cover Pull-ups or Chin-ups, Goblet Squat, Suitcase Carry, and Single Arm Overhead Press.
- Preference constraint: active A/B sessions avoid lying on the back. Supine work is allowed once weekly as a single floor block with Supine Pelvic Tilts, Glute Bridge Exercise, and Half Turkish Get-up.

## Morning Floor Draft

Cap: 15 minutes.

Purpose: wake up the body, practice joints and balance, and add small safe bone-loading signals without turning every morning into a workout.

Template:

1. Spine wake-up: cat-cow, 2 minutes.
2. Down-dog plank push-up flow, 4 minutes.
3. Bodyweight squat pattern, 3 minutes.
4. Front plank or bird-dog/modified-side-plank core block, 4 minutes.
5. Standing Calf Raises - Wall, 2 minutes.

Safety rule:

If known osteoporosis, osteopenia, fracture history, unexplained bone pain, or high fall risk is present, keep the protocol conservative and get clinician guidance before adding harder loading.

## Movement Pattern Checklist

Each week should include:

- squat
- posterior-chain control
- push
- back/posture
- trunk anti-extension
- lateral trunk
- posture/back extension
- safe weight-bearing or impact exposure
- down-dog/plank/push-up flow
- core anti-extension
- lateral core stability

## Decision Metrics

Track weekly:

- sessions completed
- movement quality
- range of motion
- balance difficulty
- soreness
- pain signals
- running recovery
- hot yoga recovery
- weight-bearing minutes
- morning floor completion
- posture/back work
- core benchmark progress
- whether the routine feels repeatable

## Falsifiers

The thesis is weakened if:

- two sessions are too little to maintain function
- two sessions are too much when combined with running and hot yoga
- mobility work is too vague to change anything
- apartment-only back/posture work is inadequate
- progression creates soreness that harms cardio adherence
- morning bone-loading work creates joint pain or feels unsafe

## Research Needed

- Resistance training dose for health and function.
- Bodyweight training effectiveness compared with other resistance tools.
- Balance and neuromotor training recommendations for adults.
- Mobility and flexibility dose guidance.
- How strength training interacts with running recovery.
- Bone mineral density evidence for running, walking, impact, and resistance training.
