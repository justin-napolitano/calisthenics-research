# Cardio Thesis

Date: 2026-06-08

## Working Thesis

For heart health, the best starting cardio plan is not maximum running. It is the highest sustainable weekly aerobic dose that fits recovery, joints, schedule, and enjoyment.

The first target should be:

- meet the guideline floor every week
- build toward the upper guideline range
- keep one fixed Wednesday 5K
- use easy walking, walk-jogging, biking, or other low-impact work to fill volume
- avoid adding intensity until volume and recovery are stable
- track weight-bearing cardio separately from non-weight-bearing cardio for bone-health purposes

## Definition Of "Optimal"

In this repo, optimal means the best benefit-to-burden range for long-term health.

It must balance:

- cardiovascular benefit
- all-cause mortality evidence
- injury risk
- recovery
- adherence
- time cost
- compatibility with calisthenics and hot yoga
- bone-loading value when relevant

Optimal does not mean the most miles, fastest 5K, or highest possible weekly training load.

## Testable Claims

### CT-001: The weekly floor should be 150 moderate-equivalent minutes.

Adults should meet at least 150 minutes of moderate aerobic activity, 75 minutes of vigorous activity, or an equivalent mix.

Status: supported by guidelines.

### CT-002: The first "optimal health" target should be 180 to 240 moderate-equivalent minutes.

This target is above the minimum but below the point where schedule and recovery become the main problem. It should be tested for 4 weeks before increasing.

Status: working hypothesis.

### CT-003: The aspirational range is 300 moderate-equivalent minutes if recovery stays clean.

Guidelines and dose-response evidence suggest additional benefit above the minimum, but the practical test is whether the user can recover, avoid injury, and sustain the plan.

Status: partially supported.

### CT-004: The Wednesday 5K should be the only planned hard run at first.

The weekly 5K provides a vigorous anchor. Additional work should mostly be easy aerobic volume unless logs show clean recovery.

Status: working hypothesis.

### CT-005: Running is a tool, not the entire cardio plan.

Walking, biking, and other low-impact aerobic work can help hit the heart-health dose with less orthopedic cost.

Status: supported by guidelines, implementation is personal.

### CT-006: Cardio minutes are not interchangeable for bone health.

Running, brisk walking, stairs, and similar weight-bearing work support bone loading more than cycling or swimming. Non-weight-bearing cardio can be excellent for heart health but should not be treated as enough for bone-health goals.

Status: supported directionally.

## First Four-Week Test

Weekly targets:

| Week | Moderate-Equivalent Minutes | Running Anchor | Extra Cardio |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | 150-180 | Wednesday 5K | Easy walk or bike sessions |
| 2 | 160-200 | Wednesday 5K | Saturday easy cardio |
| 3 | 180-220 | Wednesday 5K | Add walking volume first |
| 4 | 180-240 | Wednesday 5K | Maintain if recovery is clean |

Do not add speed work during the first 4 weeks.

## Decision Metrics

Track weekly:

- total moderate-equivalent minutes
- vigorous minutes
- running minutes
- walking or low-impact minutes
- weight-bearing cardio minutes
- non-weight-bearing cardio minutes
- Wednesday 5K effort
- lower-leg soreness
- foot, ankle, knee, hip, or back pain
- sleep and fatigue
- desire to repeat the week

## Falsifiers

The thesis is weakened if:

- the plan consistently causes lower-leg pain
- the 5K becomes a weekly race that harms recovery
- easy cardio starts displacing calisthenics, sleep, or yoga
- adherence drops below 80 percent
- fatigue trends upward for more than 2 weeks

## Research Needed

- Dose-response literature for total physical activity and mortality.
- Running-specific dose-response literature.
- Evidence comparing running with mixed aerobic work for health outcomes.
- Injury risk literature for novice or returning runners.
- Practical intensity tracking: talk test, RPE, heart rate, or pace.
