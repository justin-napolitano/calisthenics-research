# Literature Source Notes: Equilibrium Thesis

Date: 2026-06-08

## Scope

These notes test the thesis that martial movement can be studied as equilibrium management: preserving one's own balance while disrupting another person's balance, posture, base, or recovery options.

## Balance And Postural Control

### Horak, 2006: Postural Orientation And Equilibrium

Source: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16926210/

Horak frames postural control as a complex sensorimotor skill rather than a static reflex stack. The two major goals are postural orientation and postural equilibrium. Postural equilibrium concerns coordination of movement strategies that stabilize the body's center of mass during self-initiated and externally triggered disturbances.

Use for thesis:

- Strongly supports the idea that balance is dynamic.
- Supports a martial framework based on perturbations, not fixed poses.
- Suggests that voluntary action and external disturbance both matter.

Open question:

- Clinical fall-prevention literature does not automatically translate to martial conflict. We need careful bridging.

### Horak And Macpherson, 1995: Postural Orientation And Equilibrium

Source: https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1002/j.2040-4603.1995.tb01704.x

The review organizes posture around behavioral goals, biomechanical principles, postural strategies, sensory control, and central neural control. It explicitly discusses triggered reactions to external disturbances and anticipatory postural adjustments.

Use for thesis:

- Gives a rigorous vocabulary for postural orientation, equilibrium, sensory integration, and disturbance response.
- Provides a better foundation than mystical language for discussing "center," "rooting," or "connection."

### Winter, 1995: Human Balance And Posture Control During Standing And Walking

Source: https://www.scienceopen.com/document?vid=2c33a4ff-9312-420f-b536-f03a15589547

Winter's review is a classic biomechanics reference on standing and walking balance. Search records and secondary pages identify the Gait & Posture article as a heavily cited source on human balance and posture control.

Use for thesis:

- Supports center-of-mass and base-of-support framing.
- Important for connecting static stance, stepping, running, and martial footwork.

Open question:

- Need full-text review before quoting or making detailed claims.

### Pollock et al., 2000: What Is Balance?

Source: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10945424/

Pollock et al. note that "balance" is widely used but lacks a universally accepted definition across clinical specialties.

Use for thesis:

- Warns against pretending the thesis has a single simple definition of balance.
- Supports defining terms explicitly inside this repo.

## Judo, Kuzushi, And Throwing

### Three-Dimensional Center Of Mass Analysis Of Judo Throws

Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3863919/

The article describes judo throwing phases as kuzushi, tsukuri, and kake. It notes that kuzushi is commonly defined as breaking balance or preparing a throw, while also pointing out that judo biomechanics has limited quantifiable data.

Use for thesis:

- Strongly supports judo as the first martial literature domain to study.
- Reinforces that "break balance" is both a traditional concept and a biomechanical problem.

Critical note:

- The article warns that kuzushi may not always appear as simple visible balance loss; it may elicit resistance or prepare later action.

### Harai-Goshi Under Competitive And Non-Competitive Conditions

Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3809049/

This study also describes judo throws through kuzushi, tsukuri, and kake, and points toward differences between cooperative and competitive contexts.

Use for thesis:

- Supports the user's distinction between willing participants and pressure.
- Suggests that techniques must be studied under different resistance levels.

## Aikido, Least Harm, And Redirection

### Affordances In Interaction: The Case Of Aikido

Source: https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/10407413.2017.1409589

This article describes Aikido as a soft art emphasizing reciprocity, noncompetitiveness, and renouncing violence. It also describes the defender stepping out of the attack line, blending with the attack, and dispersing or redirecting energy to break balance.

Use for thesis:

- Supports Aikido as a philosophical and interactional model for least-harmful movement.
- Supports studying Aikido as balance disruption and redirection rather than mysticism.

Critical note:

- Aikido practice often assumes a cooperative learning relationship. That is valuable for tiers 1 and 2, but not sufficient evidence for high-resistance self-defense claims.

### Antagonistic Coregulation In Aikido

Source: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0167945717309235

The abstract describes Aikido as redirecting aggressive energy to break balance while preserving an ethos of nonviolence, mutuality, and respect.

Use for thesis:

- Supports the idea that Aikido can be translated into interaction dynamics.
- Supports a nonviolent ideal as one tier of the framework.

Open question:

- Need more detail before relying on the paper beyond its abstract.

## Skill Acquisition And Pressure Testing

### Combat Sports And The Constraints-Led Approach

Source: https://revpubli.unileon.es/index.php/artesmarciales/article/view/8380

This theoretical article applies ecological dynamics and the constraints-led approach to combat sports initiation. It criticizes overly cognitive ideas of "knowing how to fight" and proposes combat motor literacy and combat dexterity through modified games and constraints.

Use for thesis:

- Supports moving away from fixed technique catalogs.
- Supports testing movement ideas through constrained live problems.

### Constraints-Led Training In Interceptive Sports

Source: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1747954118812461

This systematic review found that many studies reported positive effects from constraints-led training in interceptive sports, though the evidence base is not absolute.

Use for thesis:

- Supports constraints-led training as a research-backed direction.
- Does not directly prove combat sport effectiveness, so combat-specific evidence remains needed.

## Provisional Interpretation

The thesis is plausible but should be narrowed:

> Martial movement can often be studied as equilibrium management: preserve my own postural control while disrupting the other person's base, center of mass, posture, timing, perception, or recovery options.

The stronger claim that all useful martial movement reduces to balance-breaking is not yet proven. Striking, pain, fear, weapons, surprise, and multiple-attacker contexts may operate partly through equilibrium but not only through equilibrium.
