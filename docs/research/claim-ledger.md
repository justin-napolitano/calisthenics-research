# Claim Ledger

## C-001: The baseline should include both aerobic activity and muscle-strengthening.

Status: supported

Sources:

- CDC Adult Activity Overview: https://www.cdc.gov/physical-activity-basics/guidelines/adults.html
- HHS Physical Activity Guidelines: https://odphp.health.gov/our-work/nutrition-physical-activity/physical-activity-guidelines/current-guidelines

Implication:

The repo should not treat calisthenics strength work as the entire health baseline. Walking or other aerobic work belongs in the plan.

## C-002: Strength work should cover the major muscle groups.

Status: supported

Sources:

- CDC Adult Activity Overview: https://www.cdc.gov/physical-activity-basics/guidelines/adults.html
- ACSM Resistance Training Infographic: https://www.acsm.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/03/Resistance-Training-Position-Stand-infographic.pdf

Implication:

The first circuit should include squat, hinge, push, pull/posterior chain, trunk, and lower-leg work.

## C-003: Bodyweight and home-based resistance training are acceptable seed tools.

Status: supported

Sources:

- ACSM Resistance Training Infographic: https://www.acsm.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/03/Resistance-Training-Position-Stand-infographic.pdf
- Cleveland Clinic Calisthenics Overview: https://health.clevelandclinic.org/calisthenics

Implication:

The repo can begin without a gym. The pulling pattern remains the main equipment gap.

## C-004: Beginner full-body resistance sessions should start at 2 to 3 nonconsecutive days per week.

Status: supported

Sources:

- NSCA Training Frequency: https://www.nsca.com/education/articles/kinetic-select/determination-of-resistance-training-frequency/
- ACSM Resistance Training Infographic: https://www.acsm.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/03/Resistance-Training-Position-Stand-infographic.pdf

Implication:

The daily routine should distinguish hard days from easy baseline days.

## C-005: Warm-up and cool-down should use lower intensity movement.

Status: supported

Sources:

- Mayo Clinic Warm-Up And Cool-Down: https://www.mayoclinic.org/healthy-lifestyle/fitness/in-depth/exercise/art-20045517

Implication:

Each session starts with gradual movement and ends by returning intensity toward normal.

## C-006: The seed routine should be reviewed from logs before progression.

Status: working assumption

Sources:

- Inferred from the repo goal and conservative training progression principles in ACSM and NSCA sources.

Implication:

Do not change the routine until there are 14 days of adherence, effort, soreness, and pain data unless there is a safety signal.

## C-007: The cardio target should be 150 to 300 moderate-equivalent minutes per week.

Status: supported

Sources:

- American Heart Association Physical Activity Recommendations: https://www.heart.org/en/healthy-living/exercise-and-physical-activity/fitness-basics/aha-recs-for-physical-activity-in-adults
- MedlinePlus Exercise Dose: https://medlineplus.gov/howmuchexercisedoineed.html
- CDC Adult Activity Overview: https://www.cdc.gov/physical-activity-basics/guidelines/adults.html

Implication:

The Wednesday 5K should be part of a weekly cardio target, not the entire heart-health plan.

## C-008: Low running doses can support heart-health goals, so the seed plan does not need high mileage.

Status: supported

Sources:

- Lee et al., JACC 2014: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25082581/
- Pedisic et al., BJSM 2020: https://bjsm.bmj.com/content/54/15/898

Implication:

Start with the fixed Wednesday 5K plus one easy run or walk-jog day. Use walking or low-impact cardio for additional aerobic volume.

## C-009: Hot yoga should be treated as mobility, balance, stress-management, and heat exposure unless the log shows sustained cardio intensity.

Status: supported

Sources:

- NCCIH Yoga Overview: https://www.nccih.nih.gov/health/yoga-effectiveness-and-safety
- Mayo Clinic Hot Yoga Overview: https://sportsmedicine.mayoclinic.org/news/what-is-hot-yoga/

Implication:

Keep Thursday hot yoga in the weekly plan, log hydration and heat response, and avoid stacking extra hard work around it by default.

## C-010: Balance should be treated as dynamic postural control rather than a static pose.

Status: supported

Sources:

- Horak 2006: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16926210/
- Horak and Macpherson 1995: https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1002/j.2040-4603.1995.tb01704.x
- Winter 1995: https://www.scienceopen.com/document?vid=2c33a4ff-9312-420f-b536-f03a15589547

Implication:

The framework should not talk about balance as a pose someone has or loses. It should talk about postural control under internal and external perturbation.

## C-011: The equilibrium thesis is plausible if equilibrium includes balance, posture, base, timing, perception, and recovery options.

Status: partially supported

Sources:

- Horak 2006: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16926210/
- Judo center-of-mass throw analysis: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3863919/
- Aikido affordances article: https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/10407413.2017.1409589

Implication:

The thesis should be revised away from "all martial movement breaks balance" toward "martial movement often manages equilibrium under pressure."

## C-012: Judo kuzushi is the strongest first martial literature anchor for balance-breaking mechanics.

Status: supported

Sources:

- Judo center-of-mass throw analysis: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3863919/
- Harai-Goshi competitive/non-competitive comparison: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3809049/

Implication:

Start martial literature review with judo before making broader claims about Aikido, grappling, boxing, or Muay Thai.

## C-013: Aikido supports the least-harm and redirection inspiration, but high-pressure effectiveness claims require resistance testing.

Status: partially supported

Sources:

- Aikido affordances article: https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/10407413.2017.1409589
- Aikido antagonistic coregulation abstract: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0167945717309235
- Combat sports constraints-led approach: https://revpubli.unileon.es/index.php/artesmarciales/article/view/8380

Implication:

Aikido belongs in the project as an ethical and interactional model, but not as proof that low-harm techniques work under high resistance.

## C-014: Skill claims should be tested through representative constraints before becoming public recommendations.

Status: supported

Sources:

- Combat sports constraints-led approach: https://revpubli.unileon.es/index.php/artesmarciales/article/view/8380
- Constraints-led training systematic review: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1747954118812461

Implication:

Future drills should be tested under progressively more representative resistance before the site presents them as effective outside cooperative practice.

## C-015: Cardio optimality should be treated as a benefit-to-burden range rather than a single universal number.

Status: supported

Sources:

- HHS Physical Activity Guidelines: https://health.gov/paguidelines/second-edition/pdf/Physical_Activity_Guidelines_2nd_edition.pdf
- AHA Physical Activity Recommendations: https://www.heart.org/en/healthy-living/exercise-and-physical-activity/fitness-basics/aha-recs-for-physical-activity-in-adults
- Lee et al. 2022: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9378548/
- Arem et al. 2015: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4451435/

Implication:

The plan should test a range and use logs to decide whether more volume is actually better for this user.

## C-016: A first personalized cardio target of 180 to 240 moderate-equivalent minutes per week is a reasonable hypothesis to test.

Status: working assumption

Sources:

- HHS Physical Activity Guidelines: https://health.gov/paguidelines/second-edition/pdf/Physical_Activity_Guidelines_2nd_edition.pdf
- AHA Physical Activity Recommendations: https://www.heart.org/en/healthy-living/exercise-and-physical-activity/fitness-basics/aha-recs-for-physical-activity-in-adults
- Lee et al. 2022: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9378548/

Implication:

Start above the minimum but below the likely recovery and schedule ceiling, then review after 4 weeks.

## C-017: A health-first calisthenics floor should include 2 focused sessions per week covering all major muscle groups.

Status: supported

Sources:

- HHS Physical Activity Guidelines: https://health.gov/paguidelines/second-edition/pdf/Physical_Activity_Guidelines_2nd_edition.pdf
- ACSM 2026 Resistance Training Infographic: https://www.acsm.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/03/Resistance-Training-Position-Stand-infographic.pdf

Implication:

The starting calisthenics plan should use 2 full-body sessions, not a bodybuilding split or daily hard training.

## C-018: Mobility and balance should be first-class goals in the calisthenics plan, but exact personal dose requires testing.

Status: partially supported

Sources:

- WHO Physical Activity Guidelines: https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/9789240014886
- CDC Adult Activity Overview: https://www.cdc.gov/physical-activity-basics/guidelines/adults.html

Implication:

Mobility and balance should be logged explicitly, but the repo still needs better adult-specific dose research before making stronger claims.

## C-019: Running and cardio alone are not enough for a complete bone-health plan.

Status: supported

Sources:

- NIAMS Exercise For Your Bone Health: https://www.niams.nih.gov/health-topics/exercise-your-bone-health
- Bone Health and Osteoporosis Foundation: https://www.bonehealthandosteoporosis.org/preventing-fractures/exercise-to-stay-healthy/weight-bearing/
- International Osteoporosis Foundation: https://www.osteoporosis.foundation/health-professionals/prevention/exercise/exercise-individuals-with-osteoporosis
- Mayo Clinic Press: https://mcpress.mayoclinic.org/healthy-aging/exercising-when-you-have-osteoporosis/

Implication:

Keep running and walking, but pair them with progressive resistance, posture/back work, and balance training.

## C-020: Weight-bearing cardio should be tracked separately from non-weight-bearing cardio when bone health is a goal.

Status: supported

Sources:

- NIAMS Exercise For Your Bone Health: https://www.niams.nih.gov/health-topics/exercise-your-bone-health
- Bone Health and Osteoporosis Foundation: https://www.bonehealthandosteoporosis.org/preventing-fractures/exercise-to-stay-healthy/weight-bearing/
- MedlinePlus Exercise, Lifestyle, And Your Bones: https://medlineplus.gov/ency/patientinstructions/000492.htm

Implication:

Cycling and swimming can support heart health, but they should not be counted the same as walking, stairs, jogging, or running for bone-loading purposes.

## C-021: A daily morning floor of up to 15 minutes is a reasonable place to practice mobility, balance, posture, and low-dose bone-loading work.

Status: working assumption

Sources:

- NIAMS Exercise For Your Bone Health: https://www.niams.nih.gov/health-topics/exercise-your-bone-health
- Bone Health and Osteoporosis Foundation: https://www.bonehealthandosteoporosis.org/preventing-fractures/exercise-to-stay-healthy/weight-bearing/
- Mayo Clinic Press: https://mcpress.mayoclinic.org/healthy-aging/exercising-when-you-have-osteoporosis/

Implication:

The morning floor should be treated as small daily practice, not as a replacement for focused strength sessions.
