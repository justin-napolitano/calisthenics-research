# Source Notes: Baseline Calisthenics Seed

Date: 2026-06-08

## Search Goal

Find enough credible guidance to justify a conservative daily bodyweight baseline and first logging model.

## Notes

### HHS Physical Activity Guidelines

Source: https://odphp.health.gov/our-work/nutrition-physical-activity/physical-activity-guidelines/current-guidelines

The HHS Physical Activity Guidelines for Americans are the primary U.S. public health reference for general activity targets. This source establishes the high-level evidence base rather than calisthenics-specific programming.

Use in repo:

- Anchor weekly activity targets.
- Avoid overfitting the baseline to strength only.

### CDC Adult Activity Overview

Source: https://www.cdc.gov/physical-activity-basics/guidelines/adults.html

CDC summarizes the adult target as weekly aerobic activity plus muscle-strengthening work on at least 2 days. It also names the major muscle groups: legs, hips, back, abdomen, chest, shoulders, and arms.

Use in repo:

- The baseline should include both daily movement and muscle-strengthening.
- The circuit should cover major muscle groups.

### ACSM Resistance Training Position Stand Infographic

Source: https://www.acsm.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/03/Resistance-Training-Position-Stand-infographic.pdf

ACSM's 2026 infographic says to train all major muscle groups at least 2 days per week and build gradually. It also says bodyweight, bands, and home-based training can be effective.

Use in repo:

- Bodyweight training is acceptable for the seed.
- The first program should prioritize consistency and gradual progression.

### NSCA Training Frequency

Source: https://www.nsca.com/education/articles/kinetic-select/determination-of-resistance-training-frequency/

NSCA guidance supports 2 to 3 full-body resistance sessions per week for beginners, usually on nonconsecutive days.

Use in repo:

- The routine can be daily as a habit, but focused resistance work should start at 2 to 3 nonconsecutive days.
- Easy days should not quietly become hard days.

### Mayo Clinic Warm-Up And Cool-Down

Source: https://www.mayoclinic.org/healthy-lifestyle/fitness/in-depth/exercise/art-20045517

Mayo describes warm-ups and cool-downs as lower-intensity versions of activity, with gradual ramp up and gradual ramp down.

Use in repo:

- Add a short dynamic warm-up before every session.
- Add low-intensity cooldown or walking after harder work.

### Cleveland Clinic Calisthenics Overview

Source: https://health.clevelandclinic.org/calisthenics

Cleveland Clinic presents calisthenics as bodyweight training requiring minimal equipment and gives common movement examples such as squats, push-ups, and burpees. For the seed routine, burpees are considered optional later work, not a baseline requirement.

Use in repo:

- Support bodyweight exercise selection.
- Avoid starting with high-fatigue movements as daily baseline work.

### ACE Exercise Library

Source: https://www.acefitness.org/resources/everyone/exercise-library/

ACE provides exercise reference material and variation ideas. Use as a lookup source for future movement-specific notes.

Use in repo:

- Build future exercise cards.
- Cross-check beginner variations.

## Seed Interpretation

The evidence supports a simple daily movement habit, but not hard full-body calisthenics to fatigue every day. After adding the user's fixed Wednesday 5K and Thursday hot yoga anchors, the seed routine should use 2 focused calisthenics days, 1 fixed 5K, 1 hot yoga session, easy aerobic volume around those anchors, clear stop signals, and a 14-day review before progression.
