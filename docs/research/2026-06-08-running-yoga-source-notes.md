# Source Notes: Running, Cardio, And Hot Yoga

Date: 2026-06-08

## Search Goal

Update the seed routine around fixed Wednesday night 5K and Thursday hot yoga while keeping the primary objective heart health, stability, and mobility.

## Notes

### American Heart Association Physical Activity Recommendations

Source: https://www.heart.org/en/healthy-living/exercise-and-physical-activity/fitness-basics/aha-recs-for-physical-activity-in-adults

AHA recommends at least 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity aerobic activity, 75 minutes per week of vigorous aerobic activity, or a combination. It also recommends muscle-strengthening activity at least 2 days per week, less sitting, gradual progression, and more benefit around 300 minutes per week.

Use in repo:

- Set the cardio target at 150 to 300 moderate-equivalent minutes.
- Count running as vigorous when it matches the talk-test intensity.
- Keep 2 weekly calisthenics sessions as the strength floor.

### CDC Adult Activity Overview

Source: https://www.cdc.gov/physical-activity-basics/guidelines/adults.html

CDC gives the same adult baseline: 150 minutes moderate, 75 minutes vigorous, or an equivalent combination, plus 2 days of muscle-strengthening across major muscle groups.

Use in repo:

- Keep running/cardio and calisthenics as separate weekly requirements.
- Avoid counting yoga or strength work as automatic cardio unless the intensity fits.

### MedlinePlus Exercise Dose

Source: https://medlineplus.gov/howmuchexercisedoineed.html

MedlinePlus summarizes 150 to 300 minutes moderate or 75 to 150 minutes vigorous per week and notes that physical activity should ideally be spread across several days. It also describes talk-test distinctions for moderate and vigorous intensity.

Use in repo:

- Use the talk test in the log.
- Prefer a spread-out weekly plan, while acknowledging that total weekly volume matters.

### Running Mortality Evidence

Source: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25082581/

The JACC observational study of 55,137 adults found lower all-cause and cardiovascular mortality among runners compared with nonrunners. The abstract reports that even low weekly running doses were associated with lower mortality, and that benefits were similar across running time, distance, frequency, amount, and speed quintiles.

Use in repo:

- The plan does not need high mileage to be health-oriented.
- Wednesday 5K plus one easy running or walk-jog day is a reasonable starting dose.

### Running Systematic Review

Source: https://bjsm.bmj.com/content/54/15/898

The BJSM systematic review and meta-analysis reports lower all-cause, cardiovascular, and cancer mortality risk among runners compared with nonrunners, while noting that higher running doses may not necessarily produce greater mortality benefit.

Use in repo:

- The initial goal is consistent running, not maximal mileage.
- Walking, easy cardio, and injury prevention can fill the rest of the health target.

### NCCIH Yoga Overview

Source: https://www.nccih.nih.gov/health/yoga-effectiveness-and-safety

NCCIH summarizes evidence that yoga may support stress management, mental/emotional health, sleep, and balance. It also notes that yoga is generally safe when practiced properly, though injuries can occur.

Use in repo:

- Treat Thursday yoga as valuable mobility, balance, and recovery-support work.
- Do not force extra hard calisthenics immediately before or after yoga.

### Mayo Clinic Hot Yoga Safety

Source: https://sportsmedicine.mayoclinic.org/news/what-is-hot-yoga/

Mayo describes hot yoga as vigorous and performed in warm, humid conditions. It warns that hot yoga is not for everyone, can cause heat-related illness, and should be stopped if dizziness, lightheadedness, sickness, or similar symptoms appear.

Use in repo:

- Include hydration and heat-response fields in logs.
- Treat hot yoga as a meaningful training stress even if it is not counted as running.

## Seed Interpretation

The best first plan is not more running at all costs. It is enough total aerobic volume to meet heart-health guideline ranges, anchored by a weekly 5K, with low-impact cardio filling the remaining volume and calisthenics focused on control, balance, mobility, and major muscle groups.
